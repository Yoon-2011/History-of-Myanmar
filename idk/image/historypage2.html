Queen Victoria

Queen Victoria, who ruled from 1837 to 1901, oversaw the British annexation of Burma in 1885. Her reign marked the end of the Burmese monarchy and the beginning of British colonial rule, bringing major political and cultural changes to the country.

King Edward VII

King Edward VII, who reigned from 1901 to 1910, ruled during the early years of British colonial administration in Burma. Although his reign was short and he played little direct role in Burmese affairs, the period continued the consolidation of British control and the expansion of colonial institutions in the country.

King George V

King George V, who reigned from 1910 to 1936, oversaw a period of rising Burmese nationalism under British rule. During his reign, political movements grew stronger, and in 1937 Burma was separated from British India to become a distinct colony. His era marked increasing calls for self-government among the Burmese people.

King George VI

King George VI, who reigned from 1936 to 1952, was the monarch during World War II and the Japanese occupation of Burma. His reign saw Burma’s return to British control after the war and the final steps toward independence. Burma achieved its independence in 1948 while he was still king, marking the end of British colonial rule.

Weapons

  • Martini–Henry Rifle – the primary infantry rifle.
  • Snider–Enfield Rifle – older but still used by some units.
  • Webley Revolver – standard sidearm for officers.
  • Gardner Gun – early crank-operated machine gun.
  • 7-pounder Mountain Gun – portable artillery for rough terrain.
  • Naval Gunboat Artillery – heavy guns mounted on river gunboats supporting the advance up the Irrawaddy.

Famous Governors

  • Sir Harcourt Butler (1937–1941) – Reformed administration, increased Burmese participation, managed nationalist pressures.
  • Sir Reginald Dorman-Smith (1941–1946) – Governor during WWII, oversaw evacuation, returned after Allied reconquest.
  • Sir Charles Crosthwaite (1886–1890) – First Chief Commissioner, consolidated British rule, suppressed resistance.
